3//! Time sources.
4//!
5//! Defines time-source traits and supporting types for storing, comparing,
6//! and projecting time values across different timelines.
7//!
8//! ## Core model
9//!
10//! A [`TimePoint`] defines how a concrete point representation is ordered
11//! and how to compute forward elapsed time between two such points.
12//!
13//! A [`TimeSource<P>`] produces current points of type `P` and also provides
14//! a canonical numeric projection of both points and elapsed values as `u64`
15//! together with a [`TimeScale`] describing their unit.
16//!
17//! This separates:
18//! - the **point representation** (`P`),
19//! - the **source timeline semantics**,
20//! - and the **canonical numeric view** used for diagnostics,
21//!   instrumentation, logging, and generic timestamp handling.
22//!
23//! ## Timeline model
24//!
25//! Timelines may be:
26//! - **absolute** (for example, Unix time),
27//! - **relative** (for example, boot time or JS origin),
28//! - or **synthetic** (for example, process-local or user-defined).
29//!
30//! Sources may be **monotonic** or **non-monotonic**.
31//!
32//! ## Numeric vs opaque points
33//!
34//! Some APIs expose opaque points such as [`SystemInstant`] or [`SystemTime`].
35//! These can still participate in the model by implementing [`TimePoint`],
36//! while [`TimeSource`] supplies their canonical numeric projection.
37//!
38//! Other sources use numeric point types directly, such as `u64` or `u32`,
39//! typically paired with a chosen [`TimeScale`].
40//!
41//! ## Configurable sources
42//!
43//! [`TimeSourceCfg<P>`] extends this model to source families whose behavior
44//! depends on a runtime configuration value, such as Linux clock IDs.
45//!
46//! Fixed sources automatically lift into [`TimeSourceCfg`] through a trivial
47//! `()` configuration.
48//!
49//! ## Design posture
50//!
51//! The model keeps point ordering and elapsed semantics in [`TimePoint`],
52//! while source sampling, scale metadata, and canonical numeric projection
53//! remain in [`TimeSource`] and [`TimeSourceCfg`].
54//!
55//! This allows:
56//! - opaque and numeric point representations,
57//! - compact or wide projections,
58//! - configurable clock families,
59//! - and a stable `u64` + [`TimeScale`] numeric surface
60//!   without forcing every point type itself to be numeric.
61//!
62//! ## Source comparison
63//!
64//! | Source          | Monotonic | Timeline kind | Origin / base      | Canonical point forms                |
65//! | --------------- | --------- | ------------- | ------------------ | ------------------------------------ |
66//! | `SystemTime`    | No        | Absolute      | Unix epoch         | `SystemTime`, `u64`, `u32` seconds   |
67//! | `SystemInstant` | Yes       | Synthetic     | Process-local base | `SystemInstant`, `u64`, `u32` micros |
68//! | `LinuxInstant`  | Yes       | Relative      | `CLOCK_MONOTONIC`  | `u64`, `u32` micros                  |
69//! | `LinuxTime`     | Depends   | Depends       | Selected clock     | `u64`                                |
70//! | `JsInstant`     | Yes       | Relative      | JS time origin     | `u64`, `u32` millis                  |
71//! | `TimeFakeRef`   | No        | Synthetic     | User-defined       | `u64`                                |
72//!
73//! Properties shown are semantic, not API guarantees.
74//!
75//! [`TimeScale`]: crate::TimeScale
